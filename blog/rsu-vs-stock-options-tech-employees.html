RSU vs. Stock Options: Which Is Better for Tech Employees in 2026?

April 6, 2026 · 9 min read

The short answer: RSUs are almost always better at public companies — they vest into real shares worth full market value with no action required. Stock options are potentially more valuable at early-stage startups, but only if the stock rises above your strike price. The critical difference: with 1,000 RSUs at $50/share, you receive $50,000 in taxable income at vesting. With 1,000 options at a $10 strike and $50 market price, you pay $10,000 to exercise and receive shares worth $50,000 — a $40,000 gain, taxed differently depending on ISO vs NSO. Neither is universally better — it depends on company stage, strike price vs. market price, and your own tax situation.

What RSUs and Stock Options Actually Are

Both RSUs and stock options are forms of equity compensation, but they work very differently:

RSUs (Restricted Stock Units) are a promise to deliver company shares on a vesting date. When they vest, you receive shares — or their cash equivalent — automatically. You don't pay anything to receive RSUs. The moment they vest, you own the shares outright and owe income tax on their full market value.

Stock options give you the right — but not the obligation — to purchase company shares at a fixed price (the "strike price" or "exercise price") before an expiration date, typically 10 years from grant. Options have value only if the stock price rises above your strike price. If not, they're worthless.

There are two types of stock options with very different tax treatment: Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) and Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs). We cover the ISO vs NSO tax difference in detail in our ISO vs NSO complete tax comparison.

The Real Tax Math: Side-by-Side Scenario

Let's run the actual numbers for a senior engineer named Marcus who has two job offers — one at a public tech company offering RSUs, and one at a Series B startup offering stock options.

Scenario: Marcus, Senior Software Engineer (base salary $180,000)

Offer A (Public company): 1,000 RSUs vesting over 4 years. Current stock price: $50/share. Total RSU value: $50,000 at grant (market price).

Offer B (Series B startup): 10,000 stock options with $2 strike price. Current 409A valuation: $4/share.

Now let's fast-forward 3 years. Both companies perform well:

Scenario at Year 3 RSUs (Public Co.) Stock Options (Startup)
Stock price at vest/exercise $80/share $20/share (strong growth)
Shares received (75% vested) 750 shares 7,500 options eligible to exercise
Gross value $60,000 $150,000 (at $20) — but must pay $15,000 strike
Cost to exercise $0 $15,000 (7,500 × $2 strike)
Taxable income at event $60,000 as ordinary income $135,000 spread as ordinary income (NSOs) — or AMT risk (ISOs)
Federal tax (32% marginal) ~$19,200 ~$43,200 (NSOs) or AMT on $135K spread (ISOs)
Net value after tax ~$40,800 ~$91,800 (NSOs) — but startup shares may still be illiquid

The options look better on paper — but only because this scenario assumes the startup's stock grew 10x. If the startup's stock stays at $4/share (flat), the RSUs are worth $30,000 and the options are worth $15,000 — the spread above a $2 strike. And if the company fails, options go to zero while RSUs from a public company always have market value.

When Stock Options Win

Stock options beat RSUs in one scenario: early-stage companies with a very low strike price that see dramatic stock price growth. Here's why:

At a Series A startup, your options might be struck at $0.50/share. If the company goes public at $20/share and you exercise before IPO (using an 83(b) election on early exercise), the $19.50/share gain can be taxed as long-term capital gains — which are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% rather than ordinary income rates of 22–37%.

That tax rate difference is enormous. On $200,000 in gains, ordinary income tax at 35% = $70,000. Long-term capital gains tax at 20% = $40,000. Options exercised early and held long enough can save $30,000+ in taxes on the same dollar gain.

The early exercise 83(b) strategy for startup options is covered in our 83(b) election guide. It only applies to stock options with an early exercise feature — not RSUs.

When RSUs Win

RSUs win in most situations for employees at public or late-stage companies:

  • Guaranteed value. Unlike options, RSUs never expire worthless. If the stock is $50/share on your vesting date, you receive $50/share in value. Period.
  • No cash outlay. Stock options require you to pay the strike price to exercise — which at high stock prices can mean writing a check for thousands or tens of thousands of dollars. RSUs cost you nothing.
  • No expiration pressure. Options typically expire 90 days after leaving a company, forcing you to exercise (and pay tax) on a tight deadline. RSUs simply vest — no "use it or lose it" pressure at departure. See our guide to what happens to stock options when you leave.
  • Simpler tax treatment. RSUs are taxed as ordinary income at vesting, full stop. Options have complex ISO vs NSO tax rules, AMT risk, qualifying disposition requirements, and exercise timing decisions.

The 409A Valuation Problem for Options

One thing most employees don't understand: at a startup, your option strike price is set at the company's 409A fair market value on the grant date. If you join after a Series C round at a $500M valuation, your strike price might be set at $8/share. The company needs to reach a much higher exit valuation for your options to pay off significantly.

Many employees at late-stage "unicorn" companies hold options that are technically in-the-money (stock price > strike), but by only a small margin — not enough to justify the tax and liquidity risk. A company valued at $2B on paper needs to reach $3–4B in an exit or IPO just to deliver meaningful gains above current-day option prices. RSUs given at that same stage sidestep this entirely.

The Most Important Factor: Company Stage

The rule of thumb used by equity compensation advisors:

Stage-Based Guidance

Early stage (Seed–Series B): Options are often better. Low strike prices mean large potential spreads. Early exercise + 83(b) election can convert gains to long-term capital gains. The tax upside can be massive — but so is the risk of the company failing.

Late stage (Series C+ / Pre-IPO unicorn): Neither is clearly better. Options have higher ceilings but require capital to exercise and carry AMT risk. RSUs are simpler and guaranteed. Depends on your financial situation and risk tolerance.

Public company: RSUs are almost always better. They require no upfront payment, no timing decisions, and no expiration risk. Options at public companies often have less favorable tax treatment than properly-timed option exercises at startups.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Can I negotiate whether I receive RSUs or stock options?

A: Sometimes. At startups, you can often negotiate the type of equity — some companies offer a mix or let you choose. At public companies, RSUs are standard and not usually negotiable as a type. What you can negotiate is the quantity: ask for more shares or a larger grant value rather than changing the instrument type. If you're evaluating a startup offer, it's worth asking whether options have an early exercise feature — that determines whether you can file an 83(b) election.

Q: Do RSUs or stock options have a better outcome at IPO?

A: It depends heavily on the IPO price relative to your strike price. For double-trigger RSUs that vest at IPO, you owe ordinary income tax on the full value — which can be a large bill on a big vesting event. For options exercised before IPO with an 83(b) election, gains from the exercise date to IPO may qualify as long-term capital gains. Post-IPO, RSUs from public company grants are generally cleaner. See our double-trigger RSU IPO guide for the full breakdown.

Q: What happens to my options if the company never IPOs?

A: Your options are likely worthless unless there's a secondary market or acquisition. This is the fundamental risk of startup options. If the company is acquired below your strike price, options receive nothing (common stockholders are last in liquidation preference stacks after preferred investors). RSUs in a private company have the same liquidity problem but convert to cash or acquiring-company shares in most acquisitions — providing some exit value even in moderate outcomes.

Q: How do I compare RSU and option grants when evaluating a job offer?

A: Use the expected value method: estimate the probability-weighted value at your time horizon. For options: (current value above strike) × (probability company reaches exit). For RSUs: current market value × (probability company maintains or grows value). At public companies, RSUs are worth their current market value — simple. At startups, option value is speculative. A 10,000-option grant at a startup with a $2 strike is worth $0 if the company fails and potentially $200,000+ if it succeeds. Most financial advisors suggest treating startup equity as a lottery ticket — don't depend on it.

Q: Are there tax advantages to choosing NSOs over ISOs?

A: ISOs have potentially better tax treatment — no ordinary income tax at exercise (though AMT may apply), and if you hold long enough, gains convert to long-term capital gains. NSOs are simpler: you owe ordinary income tax on the spread at exercise. However, ISOs come with the AMT trap. If you exercise a large batch of ISOs in a year where the stock price is high, you may owe Alternative Minimum Tax even if you can't sell the shares yet. ISOs are generally better for employees who can hold the stock long-term and manage AMT risk. Our AMT guide covers how to calculate your AMT exposure before exercising.
