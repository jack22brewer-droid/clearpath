How Much Tax Do You Owe When Pre-IPO Stock Vests?

April 1, 2026  ·  10 min read

The quick answer: It depends on your equity type. Pre-IPO RSUs are taxed as ordinary income (22–37% federal) at vesting — but because shares aren't tradeable, you owe real cash taxes on illiquid paper wealth. ISOs trigger no ordinary income at exercise, but the spread is an AMT preference item and can trigger an AMT bill up to 28% of the spread. NSOs trigger ordinary income at exercise equal to the spread between FMV and strike price. On a $200,000 pre-IPO equity event, total federal and state tax could range from $0 (strategic ISO exercise) to over $90,000 (NSO exercise in a high-tax state).

Why Pre-IPO Equity Tax Is a Different Problem

At a public company, tax planning is uncomfortable but manageable. When your RSUs vest at Google or Amazon, you get taxable income and your brokerage account fills up with liquid shares you can immediately sell to cover the tax bill. It's painful, but it's automatic.

Pre-IPO equity is fundamentally different. Your shares may be worth a fortune on paper — valued by the latest venture funding round — but you can't sell them. There's no public market. Yet the IRS may still want its cut. This creates the signature pre-IPO tax trap: owing real cash on illusory wealth.

The severity of this problem depends on which type of equity you hold: RSUs, ISOs (Incentive Stock Options), or NSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options). Each works differently. Each is taxed differently. And each requires a completely different planning approach.

Pre-IPO RSUs: The Illiquid Ordinary Income Problem

Most people think of RSUs as a public-company benefit. But some pre-IPO companies — especially late-stage startups with high valuations — grant RSUs to employees. The mechanics are the same as public RSUs, but the tax consequence is far more dangerous.

When pre-IPO RSUs vest, the fair market value (usually based on the most recent 409A valuation) is ordinary income. Your employer withholds at the 22% supplemental rate. But if you're a mid-level engineer at a well-funded startup, the FMV might be $50 or $100 per share — creating a six-figure tax bill on shares you can't sell.

Priya is a senior engineer at a Series D startup. 10,000 RSUs vest this quarter. The most recent 409A valuation: $35/share. Taxable income: $350,000. Federal tax (37% bracket): ~$129,500. California state: ~$46,500. Total tax: ~$176,000. Priya cannot sell a single share. She needs $176,000 in cash or estimated payments — from her salary alone.

Some companies address this by providing liquidity (tender offers, secondary market transactions) concurrent with large vesting events. Others simply don't. If yours doesn't, consider whether accepting RSU grants at a high-valued pre-IPO company is worth the liquidity risk before you're committed.

One solution available to some employees: double-trigger RSUs, which vest only when both a time condition AND a liquidity event (like an IPO) are met simultaneously. This structure defers the tax event until you can actually sell shares. Ask your startup's equity team whether your grants are single-trigger or double-trigger.

ISOs: No Tax at Exercise — But AMT Is Waiting

Incentive Stock Options are the most tax-advantaged equity form available to pre-IPO employees. The basic promise: you can exercise ISOs and owe zero ordinary income tax at exercise. You only owe tax when you sell — and if you hold long enough, that sale is taxed at long-term capital gains rates (15–20%).

The catch: the spread between your exercise price and FMV at exercise is an AMT preference item. If it's large enough, it triggers the Alternative Minimum Tax.

For 2026, the AMT exemption is $90,100 for single filers (phaseout begins at $500,000). The AMT rate is 26% on the first $232,600 of AMT income above the exemption, and 28% above that. Once your ISO spread exceeds the exemption, you're paying a parallel tax calculation and owing whichever is higher — regular tax or AMT.

Pre-IPO RSU (single-trigger) Ordinary income on full FMV LTCG on appreciation No High — tax bill on unsellable shares

The ISO Early Exercise Strategy: Filing an 83(b) Election

For ISOs (and some NSOs) at early-stage startups, the best tax move is usually to exercise early — when the 409A valuation is still close to the strike price, making the spread tiny or zero. Combined with an 83(b) election filed within 30 days of exercise, you can lock in your cost basis at the current (low) valuation and start your long-term capital gains holding period immediately.

Here's why this is so powerful:

Early Exercise Example: David, Engineer at Seed-Stage Startup

Options granted: 100,000 ISOs Strike price: $0.10/share
FMV at exercise (409A valuation) $0.12/share
AMT spread (exercise today) $2,000 ($0.02 × 100,000)
Tax owed today (below AMT exemption) ~$0
Company IPOs 3 years later at $25/share Value: $2,500,000
Tax on sale (LTCG 20% on $2,490,000 gain) ~$498,000 (vs $925,000+ as ordinary income)

The difference between exercising early with an 83(b) election versus waiting until IPO and treating the full gain as ordinary income can be hundreds of thousands of dollars on the same equity grant. NSOs: Ordinary Income You Can't Defer

Non-Qualified Stock Options trigger ordinary income tax at exercise — the spread between strike price and FMV is taxed just like salary. Unlike ISOs, there's no AMT treatment, but there's also no way to avoid ordinary income rates by holding the options longer.

For pre-IPO employees with NSOs, the planning strategy is usually to exercise in tranches — exercising enough options each year to stay within a favorable tax bracket rather than exercising everything at once and pushing a massive spread into the 37% bracket.

Quarterly Estimated Taxes: Your Cash Flow Lifeline

Whatever equity type you hold, if you expect a significant pre-IPO tax event this year, you need quarterly estimated tax payments. The IRS imposes underpayment penalties if you owe more than $1,000 at filing and didn't pay in estimates.

2026 estimated tax due dates: April 15, June 16, September 15, and January 15, 2027. Each payment should cover approximately 25% of your expected annual tax liability. If your equity situation changes mid-year (a new vesting cliff, tender offer, or secondary sale), recalculate immediately.

What Happens to Your Options if the IPO Happens Faster Than Expected?

When your company files for an IPO, timing changes. Most companies impose a 180-day lockup period post-IPO, meaning employees can't sell for six months after trading begins. During this window, any vesting RSUs become taxable — at the new, presumably much higher, public market price. This is when tax surprises peak.

If an IPO is 6–18 months away, the best moves are to exercise ISOs now (to start the LTCG clock and limit AMT exposure at lower valuations), model the post-IPO RSU tax bill against your salary income, and set aside reserves.

If your company has already filed an S-1, the window for proactive planning has mostly closed. At that point the goal shifts to managing the lockup period and selling strategically during trading windows post-lockup. Frequently Asked Questions

Do I owe taxes on pre-IPO RSUs if I can't sell the shares?

Yes — if your RSUs are single-trigger (vest on a time schedule only), the IRS taxes the fair market value at vesting as ordinary income, even if there's no public market and you can't sell. You owe real cash taxes on paper gains. If your RSUs are double-trigger, both a time condition and a liquidity event (like an IPO) must occur, so the tax event is deferred until you have liquidity. Always confirm which structure your grants use.

What is the AMT exemption for 2026?

For 2026, the AMT exemption is $90,100 for single filers (phaseout begins at $500,000 of AMTI). The AMT rate is 26% on AMT income up to $232,600 and 28% above that. ISO spreads count as AMT preference items — if your ISO spread exceeds the exemption amount, you likely owe AMT. Running a projection before exercising ISOs is critical to understanding your true tax cost.

Can I exercise ISOs before an IPO to save on taxes?

Yes — early exercise of ISOs when the 409A valuation is low (and therefore the spread is small) is one of the most powerful tax strategies for startup employees. When combined with an 83(b) election filed within 30 days, you start your LTCG holding period at exercise and owe minimal or no AMT on the tiny spread. The risk is that you pay cash to exercise shares that may become worthless if the company fails. Exercise only what you can afford to lose.

What is a 409A valuation and how does it affect my taxes?

A 409A valuation is an independent appraisal of your company's common stock fair market value, required by the IRS for setting strike prices on options. It determines the FMV used to calculate your tax liability when you exercise options or vest RSUs. Companies typically update their 409A every 12 months or after a major funding round. A higher 409A means a larger taxable spread when you exercise; a lower 409A means a smaller spread and therefore a smaller tax bill.

What happens to my unvested options if my startup gets acquired before an IPO?

Acquisition treatment of unvested equity varies by deal structure. In a stock-for-stock merger, unvested options may be converted to unvested options in the acquirer — continuing on the same vesting schedule. In a cash acquisition, unvested options might be accelerated (single or double-trigger acceleration), paid out in cash, or cancelled, depending on your grant agreement. Read your option agreement carefully, and see our article on what happens to options when you leave for related context.
