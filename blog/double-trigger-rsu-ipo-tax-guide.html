How Much Tax Do You Pay on Double-Trigger RSUs at IPO?

Published: April 5, 2026 Reading time: 8 minutes

Quick Answer

When double-trigger RSUs vest at IPO, the entire fair market value (FMV) on the vesting date is taxed as ordinary income. For a concrete example: 10,000 RSUs at $25/share = $250,000 in taxable income. Federal tax ranges from 22% to 37% depending on your total income, plus state taxes (0-13.3% in high-tax states). In this scenario, you could owe $55,000–$92,500 in combined federal and state income taxes on the vesting event alone.

Understanding Double-Trigger RSUs

Restricted Stock Units (RSUs) with double-trigger vesting are common at pre-IPO tech companies. Unlike single-trigger RSUs that vest based solely on time-based or performance conditions, double-trigger RSUs require two events: (1) your continued employment through a vesting schedule, and (2) a liquidity event such as an IPO or acquisition. When both triggers occur—typically when the company goes public—all remaining RSUs vest immediately at once.

This immediate, large-scale vesting creates a significant tax event that many employees underestimate. Understanding the tax mechanics is crucial for financial planning and avoiding surprises at tax time.

The Tax Treatment of Double-Trigger RSUs at IPO

The IRS treats RSU vesting as a taxable event. At the moment your RSUs vest, you have ordinary income equal to the fair market value of the stock on the vesting date, less any amount you paid to acquire the RSUs (usually $0 for most employees).

Example: Sarah's Double-Trigger RSU Vesting

Situation: Sarah works at a pre-IPO tech startup. She received 8,000 RSUs with a 4-year vest and double-trigger acceleration. On IPO day, these RSUs vest when the stock opens at $30/share.

Taxable Income: 8,000 shares × $30 = $240,000

Tax Owed (estimated):

  • Federal income tax (32% bracket + FICA): ~$77,000
  • California state tax (9.3%): ~$22,320
  • Total estimated tax: ~$99,320

Tax Rates and Federal Brackets

The amount of federal income tax you owe on double-trigger RSUs depends on your total taxable income for the year. For 2026, the federal marginal tax brackets are:

Most employees receiving substantial double-trigger RSUs will land in the 24%–37% federal brackets. Additionally, you owe 15.3% in FICA taxes (Social Security and Medicare) on employment income, though the Social Security portion caps at $168,600 (2026) in annual earnings.

State Income Tax Considerations

State income tax on double-trigger RSUs varies dramatically by location. Employees in no-income-tax states (Texas, Florida, Washington, Wyoming, Nevada, South Dakota, Tennessee, Alaska) owe only federal taxes. However, residents of high-tax states face substantial additional liability:

  • California: 9.3% on income over $63,398 (2026), plus Medicare surtax
  • New York: 6.85% at the state level, plus NYC tax up to 3.876%
  • Massachusetts: 5% flat state income tax
  • Oregon: Progressive rates up to 9.9%

Medicare Surtax and Estimated Tax Payments

If your total modified adjusted gross income exceeds $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly), you're subject to the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) on investment income. Additionally, the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax applies to wages over $200,000 (single).

When a large RSU vesting event occurs, you may owe estimated quarterly taxes. If you don't make quarterly estimated payments and your tax liability exceeds certain thresholds, the IRS may assess underpayment penalties. Most employees should remit estimated taxes within 15 days of the IPO closing or consult a tax professional about safe harbor provisions.

How RSU Vesting Differs from Stock Options

Unlike Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) or Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs), RSUs offer no special tax treatment. You cannot defer taxation until you sell the stock. At vesting, you recognize ordinary income equal to FMV, and there's no spread calculation (like with options). This makes double-trigger RSUs less tax-efficient than ISOs, but more predictable than NSOs, which can generate large gains if the stock rises rapidly.

Withholding and Cash Requirements

Most companies use net settle to cover withholding: they sell a portion of your vested shares immediately to cover your tax liability, withholding at 22% federally (or up to 37% if you request it). However, this withholding often falls short of your total tax obligation, especially if you live in a high-tax state or have other income. You may owe additional taxes when you file.

Action item: Calculate your full estimated tax liability and set aside funds to cover any shortfall. A common mistake is spending all net proceeds without reserving for additional taxes owed at filing.

Strategies to Minimize Tax Impact

1. Understand Your Withholding

Confirm with your company's finance team exactly how much they're withholding. Ask for a breakdown of federal, state, and FICA withholding. If they're withholding at a flat 22%, it likely won't cover your true tax liability.

2. Plan Estimated Tax Payments

If you receive a large vesting, you may need to make quarterly estimated tax payments. Consult a tax professional to determine if you qualify for safe harbor and to calculate required payments.

3. Consider Charitable Giving (Donor Advised Funds)

If you plan to donate, consider opening a Donor Advised Fund and contributing appreciated RSU shares (if possible) before vesting. This can create a deduction in the year of contribution, potentially offsetting a portion of your RSU income.

4. Evaluate Timing of Other Income

If you have flexibility, defer bonuses or side income to the following year to spread out your taxable income and reduce your marginal rate in the IPO year.

5. Pre-IPO Tax Planning

Before the IPO, discuss 83(b) elections with a tax professional. Some employees may have filed 83(b) elections on their RSUs (which is uncommon but possible), affecting the timing of income recognition. See our guide on 83(b) elections and RSUs for details.

Tax Filing Deadlines and Reporting

RSU vesting is reported on your W-2 in Box 1 (wages) or, in some cases, on Form 8949 and Schedule D if the company issues shares. Your employer will provide a brokerage statement or equity statement showing the FMV on vesting and the number of shares. Keep these documents for your tax filing and potential audit defense.

Key dates:

  • IPO closing: RSUs vest; vesting statement issued
  • January 31 (following year): W-2 or 1099 issued
  • April 15 (following year): Income tax return due
  • Estimated tax (if required): Due 15 days after IPO, or safe harbor rules apply

Common Tax Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not accounting for state taxes: Many employees assume federal withholding is sufficient, only to owe thousands at state tax time.
  • Forgetting about FICA taxes: Even if you have no federal tax liability (unlikely with large RSU vesting), you still owe 15.3% in FICA taxes.
  • Double-counting withholding: Don't assume that what your company withheld is your total tax. Compare to your total estimated liability.
  • Ignoring the Medicare surtax: If your income exceeds $200k, you owe an additional 3.8% NIIT plus 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax.
  • Selling immediately without tax plan: If you need to fund your tax liability, sell only what's necessary. Keep remaining shares to benefit from long-term capital gains rates on future appreciation.

What About Sales After Vesting?

Once your RSUs vest and convert to common stock, any future gains or losses are treated as capital gains or losses. If you sell within one year of vesting, it's a short-term capital gain (taxed as ordinary income). If you hold for more than one year, it qualifies as a long-term capital gain (taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income). This long-term rate is far more favorable than the ordinary income rates on the vesting event itself.

Example: Your RSUs vest at $25/share and you receive $250,000 of ordinary income. One year later, the stock is at $40/share. If you sell, you have a $7.50/share gain ($75,000 total) taxed at long-term capital gains rates (likely 15%), which is only $11,250 in tax—far better than ordinary income treatment.

Comparing Double-Trigger RSUs to ISOs and NSOs

For a detailed comparison of RSUs versus stock options, see our complete comparison of ISOs vs. NSOs. In summary:

  • Double-Trigger RSUs: Ordinary income on vesting, no spread, no special election opportunity.
  • ISOs: No income on grant or exercise (if exercise price ≥ FMV); capital gains on sale (if held 2+ years from grant, 1+ year from exercise).
  • NSOs: Ordinary income on exercise (the spread between FMV and strike price); capital gains on sale; more flexible but less tax-efficient than ISOs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Can I defer taxes on double-trigger RSU vesting?

A: No, not with a standard RSU. The moment your RSUs vest, you have ordinary income. Unlike ISOs or NSOs with special elections, RSUs offer no deferral mechanism. However, if your company allows you to not settle immediately (rare), you might delay taking possession of shares, though the IRS would likely view this as a deferral attempt. Consult a tax professional.

Q: What if the stock drops after my IPO vesting?

A: Tough luck, tax-wise. You owe ordinary income tax on the FMV on the vesting date, regardless of the stock's value later. If you sell at a loss after vesting, you can recognize a capital loss on the sale, but the ordinary income on vesting cannot be retroactively adjusted. This is a major risk of RSUs in volatile IPOs.

Q: Do I need a tax professional for double-trigger RSU vesting?

A: Strongly recommended, especially if you have substantial RSU vesting, live in a high-tax state, have other income sources, or are subject to the Net Investment Income Tax. The cost of a consultation ($300–$1,000) is typically far less than the tax savings from proper planning.

Q: How do I calculate my withholding if I have other income?

A: Your company withholds based on the RSU vesting alone, but your total tax liability depends on your total income for the year (salary, bonuses, spouse's income, etc.). If your combined income pushes you into a higher bracket, you may owe more. Use Form W-4 to adjust withholding on your salary, or make quarterly estimated tax payments for the shortfall.

Q: What if I'm vested but haven't settled/sold the shares?

A: You owe income tax on the vesting date (FMV), not on the sale date. Even if you hold the shares and never sell, you had ordinary income at vesting. However, if you hold and the price appreciates, future gains are capital gains (much more favorable).
