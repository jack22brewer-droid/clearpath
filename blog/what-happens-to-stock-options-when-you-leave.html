What Happens to Your Stock Options When You Leave a Company?

March 19, 2026 · 8 min read

When you leave a company, your vested stock options typically enter a post-termination exercise window — usually 90 days for ISOs and up to 10 years for NSOs. If you don't exercise within this window, your options expire worthless. For ISOs, you'll also lose the favorable tax treatment if you exercise more than 90 days after your last day. The decision of whether to exercise involves weighing the exercise cost, current fair market value, tax consequences (including potential AMT), and your belief in the company's future.

The Post-Termination Exercise Window

This is where the clock starts ticking and where most people get caught off guard. When you leave a company, your vested options don't disappear immediately — but they're on a countdown. For ISOs (Incentive Stock Options), the standard is 90 days from your last day of employment. For NSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options), the window varies wildly depending on your company's plan, but 10 years is common.

Here's the thing nobody tells you: that 90-day window for ISOs is a trap disguised as a grace period. You're dealing with leaving a job, potentially starting a new one, and suddenly there's a ticking clock on a five or six-figure financial decision. The company isn't being generous with 90 days — they're legally required to give it to you under Section 422 of the IRC. And those 90 days go fast.

Some newer tech companies have figured out that 90 days is cruel and have started extending ISO windows to 5, 7, or even 10 years post-termination. But here's the catch: when they extend the window, they usually convert your ISOs to NSOs in the process. You get more time, but you lose the preferential tax treatment of ISOs. It's a trade-off worth understanding.

Vested vs Unvested: What You Actually Own

Let's get clear on something fundamental: unvested options are not yours. They never were. When you leave, they evaporate. No negotiation, no second chances, no exceptions.

The standard vesting schedule in tech is four years with a one-year cliff. That means you own nothing for the first year. On your one-year anniversary, you suddenly own 25% of your grant. Then the remaining 75% vests monthly or quarterly over the next three years. So if you got 10,000 shares as part of your offer, and you leave after two years, you actually own 5,000 shares. The other 5,000 are gone.

This is critical context for the decision you're about to make. You're not deciding whether to exercise your full grant. You're deciding whether to exercise only the vested portion. Plan your exit timing accordingly if you care about vesting. Leaving one month before a cliff vests a massive chunk of your grant is one of the most expensive mistakes you can make.

ISOs vs NSOs: The Tax Implications of Leaving

This is where stock options stop being simple math and start being tax planning. And yes, it matters enormously.

ISOs (Incentive Stock Options): These get favorable tax treatment, but only if you follow the rules. When you exercise an ISO, you don't owe ordinary income tax on the spread immediately. Instead, you own the shares and can hold them for long-term capital gains treatment. But here's the catch: you must exercise within 90 days of leaving, and you must hold the shares for at least two years from the grant date and one year from the exercise date to keep the preferential treatment. If you exercise on day 91, your ISOs automatically convert to NSOs and you owe ordinary income tax on the spread.

And then there's AMT (Alternative Minimum Tax). When you exercise an ISO, the spread between your strike price and the fair market value is an AMT preference item. Depending on your income and other preferences, you might owe AMT on this amount even if you don't owe regular income tax. Let's say you have 5,000 ISOs with a $2 strike price and the company's 409A valuation is $10. Exercising costs you $10,000 out of pocket, but you've created a $40,000 taxable spread. That could trigger a five or six-figure AMT bill depending on your situation. If you can't afford to hold the shares for the required period, or if AMT is going to crush you, don't exercise.

NSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options): These are simpler but less favorable. When you exercise an NSO, you owe ordinary income tax on the spread immediately. No special treatment, no waiting period. The spread is taxed as W-2 income. For a private company where you can't sell the shares, this is a problem — you owe taxes on a profit you can't liquidate.

The Real Decision Framework

Stop and think before you exercise. Here are the questions you need to answer:

Question 1: Can you actually afford the exercise cost? That's shares × strike price. All of it. Out of pocket. If you can't, pause and seriously evaluate whether partial exercise or skipping entirely makes sense.

Question 2: What's the current fair market value? For private companies, this is the 409A valuation. For public companies, it's the stock price. That spread between strike and FMV is what you're betting on increasing further. If it's already high, exercising is expensive and risky.

Question 3: What's your tax exposure? Talk to a CPA who specializes in equity compensation. Model out the AMT scenario if you're dealing with ISOs. Know your ordinary income tax rate if they're NSOs. This isn't optional.

Question 4: Do you actually believe in the company? This matters. If you left because the company is a sinking ship, or you're skeptical about the business, don't throw money at it. Exercising options is an investment. Treat it like one.

Question 5: Is there a realistic liquidity path? For a private company, are they planning an IPO? Are there secondary markets where you could eventually sell? Is M&A a realistic possibility? If none of these seem likely in the next 5–10 years, your shares might be worth the paper they're printed on.

What I Wish Someone Had Told Me

The 90-day window is designed to pressure you into a decision when you're least equipped to make it. You're processing the emotions of leaving your job, you're probably stressing about your next move, and suddenly there's a financial decision that's way more complicated than you thought. The company isn't trying to trick you — but the structure of the incentive still works against you.

Here's what I'd do differently now: start planning six months before you think you might leave. Talk to a tax professional. Model out different scenarios — what if the valuation goes up? What if you can't afford to hold long enough for long-term gains? What if you just need to know the worst-case tax hit? Don't let the clock make the decision. Don't let your emotions during transition make the decision. Make the decision from a place of clarity and time.

And one more thing: if you can't afford to exercise, that's okay. Your options expiring isn't a moral failure. It's called risk management. The company priced in the risk that you wouldn't exercise. That's their problem, not yours.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I negotiate a longer exercise window?
Yes, some companies extend post-termination exercise windows beyond the standard 90 days for ISOs. You may see windows extended to 5, 7, or even 10 years post-termination. However, this usually comes with a trade-off: if the window is extended, the company typically converts your ISOs to NSOs to avoid tax complications. NSOs don't have the same favorable tax treatment as ISOs, but they do give you more time to exercise. It's worth negotiating this during your exit conversation if you have significant unvested options or a large existing grant.
What if I can't afford to exercise?
If you can't afford the full exercise cost, you have a few options. You can exercise a partial number of shares instead of all of them. Some companies or employees use loan arrangements or cashless exercises, where you borrow money to cover the exercise cost and immediately sell enough shares to repay the loan. But honestly, before pursuing any of these, ask yourself: does this investment make sense? If the company is unlikely to be acquired or go public, you might be throwing good money after bad.
Do I owe taxes just for exercising, even if I can't sell the shares?
Yes, this is a critical trap. For ISOs, you may owe Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) on the spread between your strike price and the fair market value at exercise, even if you never sell the shares. For NSOs, you owe ordinary income tax on that same spread at exercise. The tax is due when you exercise, not when you eventually sell. This is why many people get blindsided by a big tax bill after leaving a startup.
What happens if the company goes bankrupt after I exercise?
You lose your exercise cost and the shares become worthless. The problem is that you already paid taxes on the spread at exercise time, so you took the tax hit for a company that eventually failed. This is one reason why the decision to exercise should not be made lightly. If the company's prospects are uncertain, you may be better off letting the options expire.
