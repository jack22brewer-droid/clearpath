QSBS Tax Exclusion: How Startup Founders Can Exclude Up to $10 Million in Gains

April 6, 2026 · 8 min read

The bottom line: Under Section 1202 of the IRS tax code, founders and early employees who hold Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) for more than 5 years can exclude 100% of federal capital gains tax — up to $10 million per taxpayer, or 10 times their original investment (whichever is greater). On a $10 million gain, that's up to $2 million in federal tax eliminated. The catch: the stock must be acquired at original issuance from a C-corporation with gross assets under $50 million at the time of issuance, and you must hold it for more than 5 years. Most VC-backed startups structured as C-corps qualify automatically — but most founders don't know to verify QSBS status early.

What Is QSBS and Why Does It Matter?

Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) is one of the most powerful tax benefits in the U.S. tax code — and one of the least understood. Congress created it in 1993 to encourage investment in early-stage companies. For startup founders and early-stage employees, it can mean the difference between paying 20%+ in capital gains tax on an exit and paying nothing.

The mechanics are straightforward: if you acquire stock that qualifies under Section 1202 of the IRS code and hold it for more than 5 years, you can permanently exclude up to $10 million per taxpayer — or 10 times your adjusted basis (original investment), whichever is larger — from federal capital gains tax.

For a founder who bought 1 million shares at $0.001/share (total cost: $1,000) in a company that sells for $20/share, that's $19,999,000 in capital gains. Under QSBS, 10x the original $1,000 investment = $10,000 exclusion... but the per-person cap is $10 million, so the founder excludes $10 million entirely from federal tax. Tax savings at 20% federal capital gains rate: $2 million.

QSBS Qualification Requirements

Not all startup stock is QSBS. Here's the checklist your stock must meet at the time of acquisition:

Requirement Details Common Issues
C-corporation only Must be a domestic C-corp, not S-corp, LLC, or partnership Many startups convert from LLC to C-corp — shares issued before conversion don't qualify
Gross assets ≤ $50 million The company's aggregate gross assets must be ≤ $50 million at issuance (and immediately after) Late-stage issuances (post-Series C+) often miss this threshold
Original issuance Stock must be acquired directly from the company — not secondary market purchases Buying shares from another employee/founder on a secondary platform does NOT qualify
Held more than 5 years Must hold the stock for more than 5 years from acquisition date Selling early (even at Series D) forfeits the exclusion unless you use a rollover under Section 1045
Active business requirement At least 80% of assets used in an active qualified trade or business Certain professional services are excluded: law, health, finance, banking, insurance, hospitality
You must be a non-corporate taxpayer Must be an individual, trust, or pass-through entity — not a C-corp VCs investing through C-corp entities cannot claim QSBS

How Much Can You Actually Exclude?

The exclusion limit is the greater of $10 million or 10 times your adjusted basis in the stock. For founders who got shares at near-zero cost, the $10 million cap usually applies. For later employees or investors who paid more, the 10x basis rule can yield a larger exclusion.

Scenario: Priya, Co-Founder

Priya co-founds a SaaS company in 2020, purchasing 2 million shares at $0.001/share = $2,000 total. The company sells in 2026 for $12/share. Priya's gain: $23,998,000.

QSBS exclusion: greater of $10M or 10x basis ($2,000 × 10 = $20,000). Per-taxpayer cap = $10 million.

Federal tax on excluded $10M: $0

Federal tax on remaining $13,998,000 at 20% LTCG rate: ~$2,800,000

Without QSBS: $23,998,000 × 20% = $4,799,600 in federal tax

QSBS saves Priya approximately $2,000,000 in federal tax.

Scenario: James, Series A Employee (later hire, higher cost basis)

James joins post-Series A and exercises 100,000 options at $2/strike when 409A = $2. Total basis: $200,000. Company exits at $30/share. James's gain: $2,800,000.

QSBS exclusion: greater of $10M or 10x basis ($200,000 × 10 = $2,000,000). The 10x rule = $2M exclusion, which covers most of the $2.8M gain.

QSBS exclusion: $2,000,000. Taxable gain: $800,000. Tax at 20%: $160,000 vs. $560,000 without QSBS.

QSBS saves James approximately $400,000 in federal tax.

The 5-Year Clock and When It Starts

This is where founders often make costly mistakes. The 5-year holding period begins on the date you acquire the stock — not when it vests.

If you're an employee who received stock options, you need to exercise them to start the QSBS clock. Unexercised options are not stock — the clock doesn't start until you exercise. This is why early exercise with an 83(b) election is so powerful for startup employees: exercising early (when the 409A is low, meaning low exercise cost) starts the QSBS clock years before vesting completes.

For founders who purchased stock at incorporation, the clock typically started at incorporation — which means if you founded a company in 2020 or earlier and are still holding, you may already have QSBS that qualifies for the full 100% exclusion.

QSBS for Option Holders: The Critical Early Exercise Strategy

Most employees with stock options miss out on QSBS because they wait to exercise until close to an IPO or exit — when the 5-year clock hasn't started yet. Here's the strategy that maximizes QSBS benefit for option holders:

  1. Exercise options early — ideally in the first year of employment when the 409A valuation is low, minimizing the cost to exercise and the ordinary income recognized on NSO exercise.
  2. File an 83(b) election within 30 days for restricted stock or early-exercise options, to lock in the low tax basis and start the QSBS holding period immediately. See our complete 83(b) election guide.
  3. Verify the company's QSBS eligibility — confirm it's a C-corp with gross assets under $50 million at the time of your exercise/grant. Get written confirmation from the company's legal team.
  4. Hold for 5+ years — don't sell even in a secondary transaction before crossing the 5-year threshold.

The combination of early exercise + 83(b) election + QSBS 5-year hold is often called the "trifecta" by equity compensation advisors. It converts what would be ordinary income into either zero federal tax (QSBS) or long-term capital gains at preferred rates.

Stacking QSBS Across Family Members

The $10 million exclusion is per taxpayer. A married couple filing separately each gets their own $10 million exclusion — meaning $20 million total in excluded gains, provided each spouse owns the stock. Transfers of QSBS between spouses are generally permitted without losing QSBS status. Additionally, transferring QSBS shares to trusts or children (subject to gift tax rules) can multiply the exclusion across family members. This strategy — called QSBS stacking — is used by founders planning large exits.

Important: State tax may still apply

The Section 1202 QSBS exclusion is a federal tax benefit only. Some states don't conform to Section 1202 — notably California. If you're a California resident, you'll still owe California state income tax (up to 13.3%) on the entire gain, even if the federal gain is excluded. This is a significant caveat for Silicon Valley founders and should factor into exit planning. Consider consulting a tax advisor about state-level implications before an exit.

The Section 1045 Rollover: What to Do If You Can't Wait 5 Years

If you sell QSBS before the 5-year threshold (or if your company is acquired before 5 years), you may still preserve tax-deferred treatment using a Section 1045 rollover. Within 60 days of the sale, you can reinvest the proceeds into new QSBS — and the original stock's holding period tacks onto the new QSBS. This allows founders who receive cash in an early acquisition to defer and potentially eventually exclude the gain, as long as they reinvest into another qualified small business. The replacement stock must also independently qualify as QSBS.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Do stock options from a startup qualify as QSBS?

A: The options themselves don't qualify — QSBS requires actual stock ownership. You need to exercise the options (paying the strike price to receive shares) for the 5-year QSBS clock to start. That's why early exercise is critical. If you wait until IPO to exercise, your 5-year clock starts at IPO, meaning you'd need to hold the stock until 5 years post-IPO — which most employees don't do. ISO and NSO exercises both can result in QSBS shares, but you must verify the company still meets gross asset requirements at the time of exercise.

Q: How do I know if my company's stock qualifies as QSBS?

A: Ask your company's legal team or CFO for written confirmation of QSBS status at the time of your grant/exercise. They should be able to confirm: (1) the company is a C-corp, (2) gross assets were under $50 million at issuance, (3) the active business requirement is met. Many startup attorneys include QSBS representations in equity documentation. If you can't get confirmation, an equity tax attorney can analyze your stock certificates and company structure. Don't assume — verify before relying on the exclusion at exit.

Q: What if my company converts from an LLC to a C-corp? Do I get QSBS?

A: Stock received in exchange for LLC interests at the time of conversion is generally treated as newly issued stock and can qualify for QSBS — but only if all other requirements are met at the conversion date. Stock or equity interests issued by the LLC before the conversion do not qualify retroactively. The QSBS clock starts at the conversion/issuance date, not the original founding date. This is a common issue for companies that started as LLCs for flexibility and later converted to C-corps for VC fundraising.

Q: Can QSBS gains be excluded from the Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)?

A: Yes. Gains excluded under Section 1202 are not subject to the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) that applies to investment income for high earners (above $200K single / $250K married). This further improves QSBS economics: without QSBS, a high earner might pay 20% capital gains + 3.8% NIIT = 23.8% federal rate. With QSBS, that portion is excluded entirely. Combined with no state tax exclusion in states like Texas, Florida, or Washington, the total effective rate on QSBS gains can be near zero for founders who planned carefully.

Q: Are RSUs from a startup eligible for QSBS treatment?

A: Generally no — RSUs are not issued at original issuance in the same way as founder stock or early exercised options. RSUs vest into shares, but for QSBS purposes, the acquisition date is the vesting date (when you receive the shares), not the grant date. This means if you receive vested RSUs from a startup that has already grown past the $50 million gross assets threshold, those shares don't qualify. RSUs from early-stage companies with under $50M in assets can qualify, but the company's rapid growth often disqualifies later vest tranches. The QSBS benefit is primarily designed for founder stock and early employee options exercised while the company is still small.
