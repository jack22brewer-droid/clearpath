RSU vs ESPP: Which Gives You a Better Tax Deal?

April 1, 2026  ·  9 min read

Short answer: RSUs are taxed as ordinary income at vesting — typically 22–37% federal plus state. ESPPs are more complex: the discount (usually 15%) is taxed as ordinary income when you sell, but gains beyond that may qualify for long-term capital gains rates as low as 15% if you hold long enough. A qualifying ESPP disposition can save you 10–22 percentage points in federal taxes versus RSU vesting, but most employees miss the holding period and lose the advantage entirely.

The Core Difference: When and How Each Is Taxed

RSUs and ESPPs are two of the most common equity benefits at large tech companies — and they operate on completely different tax logic. Understanding which is more tax-efficient isn't just academic: on a $100,000 equity position, choosing the wrong strategy can cost you $15,000–$22,000 in avoidable taxes.

Let's establish what each benefit actually is before getting into the tax mechanics.

RSUs (Restricted Stock Units) are a grant of company shares that vest over time — typically a 4-year schedule with a 1-year cliff. You don't own anything until they vest. When they vest, you receive shares, and the fair market value on that day is taxed as ordinary income, just like a cash bonus.

ESPPs (Employee Stock Purchase Plans) let you purchase company stock at a discount — usually 15% — through payroll deductions. In a qualified ESPP under Section 423, you have an offering period (typically 6–24 months), a purchase date when you buy the stock at the discounted price, and then a sale date. The tax treatment depends almost entirely on when you sell.

How RSU Taxes Work: Simple but Expensive

RSU taxation has one trigger: vesting. The moment shares vest, the IRS treats the entire fair market value as ordinary income. Your employer withholds taxes — but at the flat 22% supplemental withholding rate, which is almost never enough if you're in the 32% or 37% bracket.

Here's what that looks like in practice. Imagine you're a software engineer at a large tech company, earning $180,000 in salary. You have $80,000 in RSUs vesting this year.

Sofia keeps less than 42 cents of every dollar that vested. The withholding happens automatically — but only at 22% federal. She owes an additional $12,000 at tax time unless she made estimated quarterly payments. This is the most common RSU tax trap: the surprise April bill.

After vesting, if Sofia holds the shares and they appreciate, that additional gain is taxed as capital gains — short-term (ordinary income rates) if held under a year, long-term (0%, 15%, or 20% federal) if held over a year.

How ESPP Taxes Work: More Complex, Potentially Better

ESPP taxation has two moving parts: the discount and the gain. And the IRS splits them differently depending on whether your sale is a "qualifying" or "disqualifying" disposition.

Qualifying disposition: You hold the shares for at least 2 years from the offering date AND at least 1 year from the purchase date. This is the favorable outcome.

Disqualifying disposition: You sell before meeting both holding periods. This is what most employees do — and it costs them.

The qualifying ESPP is dramatically more tax-efficient — but only if you hold. Most employees sell immediately after purchase ("same-day sale"), turning a qualifying event into a disqualifying one and wiping out the entire tax advantage.

The ESPP Holding Period Trap

Here's the math that surprises people: a 15% discount from the purchase date sounds like a guaranteed win. But consider that the "offering date" price — the lower of the stock price at the start or end of the offering period — can be set 24 months in the past. If your company's stock is volatile, the spread between what you paid and what the stock is worth on purchase day can be massive. And all of that spread is ordinary income in a disqualifying disposition.

Example: Marcus contributes $15,000 to his ESPP. The offering price was set when stock traded at $100. By purchase date, stock is at $150. He buys at $85 (15% off $100). He immediately sells at $150. His ordinary income isn't just $15 (the 15% discount) — it's $65 per share (the full spread from purchase price to FMV). On 176 shares, that's $11,440 in ordinary income — taxed at 37%. He would have been better off never contributing.

The lesson: before enrolling in an ESPP, model the worst case. If your company is volatile and you can't commit to the holding period, the guaranteed-discount marketing can be misleading.

Which Should You Prioritize?

Most financial advisors will tell you: maximize your ESPP contribution if you can hold qualifying. Here's why the math usually works:

  • The 15% guaranteed discount plus a lookback provision means your breakeven on the stock is substantially lower than market price. Even with stock appreciation risk, that's a meaningful cushion.
  • RSU taxes are unavoidable — you owe ordinary income the moment they vest, regardless of whether you sell. You can't defer them.
  • With a qualifying ESPP, you effectively convert 85% of your ESPP income into long-term capital gains, paying 15% instead of 37% on that portion.

That said, prioritization depends on your concentration risk. If both your salary and your equity are tied to the same company, holding ESPP shares for 2 years adds significant single-stock exposure. The tax savings may not justify the risk if the stock drops 30% while you're waiting to qualify.

Practical Tax-Minimization Moves

Given both instruments, here's what sophisticated equity holders do:

  • For RSUs: Sell immediately at vesting (unless you have strong conviction) to reset your cost basis. Holding only makes sense if you'd buy the stock with after-tax cash at the current price. Most people wouldn't — so most people shouldn't hold.
  • For RSUs: Make estimated quarterly tax payments equal to the difference between your 22% withholding and your actual marginal rate. If you're in the 37% bracket, that's an additional 15% of your RSU value due quarterly to avoid underpayment penalties.
  • For ESPPs: Contribute the maximum allowed (typically 10–15% of salary up to IRS limits). Model whether you can genuinely hold 1 year from purchase date and 2 years from offering date before enrolling. If the answer is no, still contribute — the 15% discount is almost always worth it even in a disqualifying disposition — but sell quickly to limit stock price risk.
  • For ESPPs: Track your offering date carefully. The 2-year clock starts from the offering date, not the purchase date. Many employees miscalculate and sell one month too early.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are RSUs or ESPPs better if my company's stock is likely to go up a lot?

If you expect significant appreciation, ESPP qualifying dispositions become even more advantageous. The growth beyond your purchase price is taxed at LTCG rates (15–20%) instead of ordinary income rates (up to 37%). RSU gains after vesting are also LTCG-eligible if held 1+ year, but the initial vest value is still ordinary income. ESPP holders get the better deal on appreciation.

What is the maximum ESPP contribution limit for 2026?

Under IRS Section 423, you can purchase a maximum of $25,000 in stock per calendar year under a qualified ESPP (measured at the offering date price, not the discounted purchase price). Many employees hit this cap and max it out — especially in high-cost-of-living tech hubs where salaries are sufficient to support the contribution level.

Do I owe taxes when RSUs vest even if I don't sell the shares?

Yes — and this surprises many employees. RSU vesting is a taxable event regardless of whether you sell. The IRS treats the fair market value on the vesting date as ordinary income. Your employer should withhold taxes, but often only at the flat 22% supplemental rate. If your marginal rate is higher, you'll owe the difference when you file.

Can I lose money on an ESPP even with a 15% discount?

Yes. If the stock drops more than 15% from your purchase price before you sell, you're in a loss position. The 15% discount provides a buffer, but it doesn't guarantee a profit. In a disqualifying disposition, your breakeven is your purchase price. In a qualifying disposition, you also need to account for the 15% discount being taxed as ordinary income at sale, which reduces your effective discount to about 9–10% after tax at high income levels.

What's the difference between an RSU and an RSA?

RSUs are promises to deliver shares in the future — you don't own anything until vesting. RSAs (Restricted Stock Awards) transfer shares to you at grant, subject to forfeiture restrictions. Because you legally own RSAs immediately, you can file an 83(b) election within 30 days of grant to be taxed on the current (usually low) value rather than the vested value. RSUs do not qualify for an 83(b) election. For more on 83(b) elections, see our full 83(b) election guide.
